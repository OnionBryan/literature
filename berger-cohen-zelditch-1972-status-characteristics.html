Status Characteristics and Social Interaction

Joseph Berger, Bernard P. Cohen, & Morris Zelditch Jr.

American Sociological Review, 1972

Abstract

Berger, Cohen, and Zelditch present the foundational theory of how informal status hierarchies emerge in task-oriented groups. Expectation States Theory explains how people unconsciously use "status characteristics"‚Äîattributes like education, occupation, gender, or race‚Äîto form performance expectations of each other. Those with high-status characteristics are given more opportunities to contribute, their contributions are evaluated more favorably, and they gain more influence over group decisions. This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy where initial status differences become performance differences, legitimizing the hierarchy.

Overview: How Status Creates Power

Expectation States Theory emerged from experimental research at Stanford showing that even in newly formed groups with no history, status hierarchies form almost immediately. These hierarchies are not based on demonstrated competence‚Äîthey form before anyone has done anything. Instead, they're based on status characteristics that members bring with them from the broader society.

The Core Insight

When people come together in a task group, they don't start as blank slates. They carry markers of social status‚Äîtheir education, occupation, age, gender, race, appearance, accent, and more. These characteristics activate expectations about competence. Without anyone deciding to be biased, the group organizes itself into a hierarchy based on these external cues.

Types of Status Characteristics

Diffuse Status Characteristics

Broad characteristics that carry general expectations about competence across many domains:

Education Level Occupation Gender Race/Ethnicity Age Physical Attractiveness

Specific Status Characteristics

Narrow characteristics relevant only to particular tasks:

Relevant Expertise Past Performance Formal Training Certifications

Why This Matters

This theory reveals a mechanism for the reproduction of inequality. Status characteristics derived from societal hierarchies get imported into every new group interaction, recreating power imbalances even in supposedly meritocratic settings. The person who gets to speak first, whose ideas get taken seriously, and who emerges as the leader is often determined before the group even begins working.

Expectation States Theory in Detail

The Formation of Performance Expectations

The theory posits that when people interact in task-oriented groups, they form expectations about each other's likely competence. These expectations have four sources:

  • Status Characteristics: External markers of social position (the focus of this theory)
  • Prior Beliefs: Existing beliefs about what types of people are competent at what tasks
  • Interaction History: Past experience with specific individuals
  • Reward Expectations: Beliefs about who deserves rewards and recognition

The Burden of Proof Process

A key concept: status characteristics become relevant to a task unless they are explicitly dissociated from it. The "burden of proof" lies with demonstrating irrelevance, not relevance. This means:

The Burden of Proof Principle

If a status characteristic could possibly be relevant to a task, people will assume it is relevant‚Äîunless actively told otherwise.

This explains why diffuse status characteristics like gender or race affect expectations even in tasks with no logical connection. Being a lawyer (high-status occupation) may influence how your opinion is weighted in a group discussion about restaurant recommendations, even though legal expertise is irrelevant.

Behavioral Consequences

Once expectations form, they systematically shape group interaction:

For High-Status Members
  • Given more opportunities to participate
  • Contributions receive more attention
  • Errors overlooked or excused
  • Opinions more likely to be adopted
  • More likely to emerge as leader
For Low-Status Members
  • Fewer opportunities to contribute
  • Contributions discounted or ignored
  • Errors noticed and remembered
  • Must work harder to be heard
  • Leadership attempts resisted

The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

The theory's most powerful insight: these expectations create their own confirmation. When high-status members are given more opportunities and encouragement, they perform better. When low-status members are ignored and discounted, they disengage or perform worse. The initial expectation becomes reality.

The Status-Performance Cycle

High Status ‚Üí High Expectations ‚Üí More Opportunities ‚Üí Better Performance ‚Üí "Proof" of Competence ‚Üí Reinforced Status

Combining Multiple Characteristics

In real groups, people possess multiple status characteristics‚Äîsome high, some low. The theory predicts that people combine these into an overall expectation state, with consistent characteristics reinforcing each other and inconsistent characteristics attenuating effects. A Black female doctor, for instance, has mixed status characteristics that may produce complex, context-dependent expectations.

Power Dynamics and Implications

Status as a Source of Power

Expectation States Theory reveals status as a distinct source of power‚Äînot power derived from formal authority, resource control, or coercion, but from others' beliefs about your competence. This form of power is particularly insidious because it operates through others' voluntary deference rather than explicit domination.

How Status Creates Power

  • Agenda Setting: High-status members' concerns become group priorities
  • Framing: Their interpretations of situations become dominant
  • Legitimate Influence: Their suggestions are adopted without resistance
  • Reduced Scrutiny: Their decisions face less questioning
  • Benefit of Doubt: Their failures are attributed to circumstances, not ability

Implications for Workplace Inequality

This theory explains persistent patterns of workplace inequality that formal policies don't address:

  • Meeting Dynamics: Who speaks, who gets interrupted, whose ideas get credit
  • Performance Evaluation: Identical work evaluated differently based on who did it
  • Promotion Decisions: "Leadership potential" seen more readily in high-status groups
  • Networking: High-status individuals attract more attention and connections
  • Mentorship: Mentors more likely to invest in high-status prot√©g√©s

Interrupting the Cycle

Research has identified several strategies for disrupting status-based expectations:

  • Demonstrate Specific Competence Early: Show task-relevant expertise before diffuse status characteristics dominate
  • Use Structural Interventions: Assign roles, rotate leadership, use round-robin contributions
  • Make Status Irrelevant: Explicitly state that background characteristics don't predict performance on this task
  • Assign Competence to Low-Status Members: Public recognition of their expertise creates counter-expectations
  • Awareness Training: Making the process visible reduces (but doesn't eliminate) its effects

The Legitimacy Trap

A troubling finding: low-status members often accept the hierarchy as legitimate. They internalize lower expectations for themselves and resist when other low-status members attempt to assert influence. This means the hierarchy is maintained not just by those at the top, but by everyone‚Äîincluding those it disadvantages.
