The External Control of Organizations

Pfeffer, J., & Salancik, G. R. (1978). Harper & Row.

"Organizations are not self-directed and autonomous; they are controlled by their environments through resource dependencies."

üìö Resource Dependence Theory: Overview

The Central Insight

Organizations are externally controlled. To survive, they must acquire resources from their environment‚Äîand this dependence creates power relationships that shape organizational behavior, structure, and strategy.

Why This Book Matters

Before Pfeffer and Salancik, organizational theory focused primarily on internal operations‚Äîefficiency, structure, management. Resource Dependence Theory (RDT) shifted attention outward: organizations are open systems that cannot be understood apart from their environments.

üí° The Key Question RDT Answers

Why do organizations adopt particular structures and take particular actions? Not primarily for efficiency, but to manage external dependencies and reduce uncertainty in resource flows.

The Power-Dependence Framework

At its core, RDT is about power. Power is relational and emerges from dependence:

The Fundamental Equation:

A's Power over B = B's Dependence on A

Power is the inverse of dependence. Those who control critical resources have power over those who need them.

üîó Interdependence

Organizations are embedded in networks of resource exchanges. No organization is truly independent‚Äîall rely on others for inputs and outputs.

‚ö° Power

Power flows to those who control critical resources. The more concentrated and important the resource, the greater the power of its controller.

‚ùì Uncertainty

Dependence creates uncertainty. Organizations work to reduce uncertainty through various strategies‚Äîfrom contracts to acquisitions to political action.

üéØ Effectiveness

Organizational effectiveness means satisfying the demands of key stakeholders who provide critical resources‚Äînot just internal efficiency.

Historical Context

Published in 1978, this book emerged at a pivotal moment:

  • Post-Contingency Theory: Building on ideas that environment shapes structure, RDT asked how this happens
  • Systems Theory: The open systems view emphasized exchange across organizational boundaries
  • Power Studies: Pfeffer's prior work on organizational power laid the foundation
  • Strategic Management: Growing interest in how organizations respond to competitive pressures
üìñ Classic Example: The Buyer-Supplier Dance

Consider two buyers competing for a supplier's limited production. This creates uncertainty‚Äîneither knows if they'll get what they need. To reduce this uncertainty, they might:

  • Sign long-term contracts (reducing allocation uncertainty)
  • Vertically integrate (acquire the supplier)
  • Diversify suppliers (reduce dependence on any one)
  • Form a joint venture (share control over the resource)

Each strategy trades some autonomy for reduced uncertainty.

Effectiveness vs. Efficiency

Pfeffer and Salancik make a crucial distinction that challenges traditional management thinking:

Dimension Efficiency Effectiveness
Focus Internal operations External relationships
Measure Output per unit input Satisfaction of stakeholder demands
Standard Technical (engineering) Social (political)
Survival Based On Cost minimization Resource acquisition
Key Question "Are we doing things right?" "Are we satisfying those who matter?"

üí° The Survival Paradox

An organization can be highly efficient yet fail because it doesn't satisfy the demands of key resource providers. Conversely, a seemingly inefficient organization can thrive if it successfully manages its dependencies.

üéØ Core Theory: The Three Determinants of Dependence

How dependent is Organization B on Organization A? Pfeffer and Salancik identify three key factors that determine the degree of dependence:

üìä 1. Resource Importance

Magnitude: What proportion of total inputs/outputs does this exchange represent?

Criticality: Can the organization continue to function without this resource?

Example: Electricity may be a small cost but is absolutely critical‚Äîoperations stop without it.

üéõÔ∏è 2. Discretion Over Allocation

How much control does the other party have over whether and how the resource is allocated?

Key aspects: possession, access, actual use, rule-making authority

Example: A sole supplier with no contractual obligations has high discretion.

üîí 3. Concentration of Control

Are there alternative sources for this resource, or is control concentrated?

High concentration = few alternatives = high dependence

Example: A rare earth mineral available from only one country creates high dependence.

The Dependence Formula:

Dependence = Importance √ó Discretion √ó Concentration

All three factors multiply together. If any is low, overall dependence is reduced.

The Enacted Environment

A critical insight from Pfeffer and Salancik: environments are not objective realities that organizations simply respond to. Instead, environments are enacted‚Äîconstructed through processes of attention, interpretation, and meaning-making.

üí° Environment as Construction

"Environments become known through a process of enactment in which perceptions, attention, and interpretation come to define the context for the organization."

This has profound implications:

  • Selective Attention: Organizations don't respond to everything‚Äîthey notice what they're trained to notice
  • Interpretation: The same environmental signal can mean different things to different organizations
  • Errors: Organizations can misread dependencies, misinterpret demands, and fail to see conflicts
  • Self-Fulfilling: How an organization perceives its environment shapes its actions, which can change the environment

Organizational Environment: Three Dimensions

Dimension Description Impact on Organization
Concentration How dispersed or concentrated is power and resources? Concentrated = clearer demands but higher dependence; Dispersed = competing demands but more alternatives
Munificence How abundant or scarce are critical resources? Scarcity increases competition and dependence; Abundance provides slack and autonomy
Interconnectedness How linked are environmental actors to each other? High interconnection means actions ripple; Low means actors can be played against each other

Power Within Organizations

RDT also explains internal organizational power. Those who can address critical organizational uncertainties gain power:

üèÜ Strategic Contingencies

Subunits that cope with the most critical uncertainties facing the organization gain the most power and resources.

üèõÔ∏è Institutionalization of Power

Once gained, power tends to persist. Powerful subunits shape structures, budgets, and norms to maintain their position even after the original uncertainty is resolved.

üìñ Example: The Rise of the CFO

In the 1970s-80s, financial markets became increasingly important for corporate survival. Organizations faced new uncertainties: hostile takeovers, shareholder activism, capital markets.

Result: Finance departments that could manage these uncertainties gained power. The CFO role expanded dramatically. Even after market conditions stabilized, finance retained its elevated position‚Äîpower had become institutionalized.

Constraints and Discretion

Organizations face constraints but also have discretion. The key is understanding the balance:

üí° The Paradox of Autonomy

Organizations gain autonomy by strategically managing dependencies. But every strategy to reduce one dependency often creates new dependencies. The goal isn't independence‚Äîit's favorable interdependence.

  • Constraint: When behavior must conform to external expectations
  • Discretion: When organizations can choose among acceptable alternatives
  • Strategic Choice: Using discretion to reshape constraints over time

‚öôÔ∏è Strategies for Managing Resource Dependence

Pfeffer and Salancik identify five major strategies organizations use to manage environmental dependencies. Each involves trade-offs between autonomy and uncertainty reduction.

The Five Strategic Options

üè¢ 1. Mergers & Acquisitions

Goal: Absorb the source of dependence

Types:

  • Vertical: Acquire suppliers or customers
  • Horizontal: Acquire competitors
  • Diversification: Reduce dependence on any single market

Trade-off: Eliminates external dependence but creates internal coordination challenges

ü§ù 2. Joint Ventures & Alliances

Goal: Share resources and reduce uncertainty without full merger

Forms:

  • Joint ventures
  • Strategic alliances
  • Long-term contracts
  • Consortia

Trade-off: Maintains some autonomy but requires coordination and trust

üëî 3. Boards of Directors (Co-optation)

Goal: Bring external stakeholders inside to secure commitment

Benefits:

  • Advice and counsel
  • Information channels
  • Preferential resource access
  • Legitimacy

Trade-off: Gains access but gives outsiders influence over decisions

üèõÔ∏è 4. Political Action

Goal: Shape the rules of the game

Tactics:

  • Lobbying for favorable regulations
  • Political contributions
  • Trade association activities
  • Public relations campaigns

Trade-off: Can reshape environment but invites scrutiny and may backfire

üë§ 5. Executive Succession

Goal: Match leadership to environmental demands

Logic: New leaders bring:

  • Different networks and relationships
  • Skills suited to current challenges
  • Signal of change to stakeholders

Trade-off: Adapts organization but disrupts internal relationships

Choosing Among Strategies

The choice of strategy depends on several factors:

Factor Favors Internal Solutions (Merger) Favors External Solutions (Alliance, Co-opt)
Uncertainty Level Very high‚Äîneed complete control Moderate‚Äîpartial control sufficient
Resource Availability Slack resources for acquisition Limited resources
Legal Environment Permissive antitrust Restrictive antitrust
Reversibility Needed Permanent solution acceptable Need flexibility
Number of Dependencies Few, critical ones Many, diverse ones

The Concept of "Bridging" vs. "Buffering"

üåâ Bridging Strategies

Create linkages with the environment:

  • Form alliances
  • Co-opt board members
  • Build relationships

Adapts the organization to fit environmental demands

üõ°Ô∏è Buffering Strategies

Protect the core from environmental turbulence:

  • Build inventory
  • Diversify
  • Vertical integration

Insulates the organization from environmental shocks

Interlocking Directorates: A Deep Dive

One of the most studied RDT phenomena is the interlocking directorate‚Äîwhen a person serves on the boards of multiple organizations.

üí° Why Interlocks Form

Interlocks are not random. They systematically form between organizations that have resource dependencies. Banks interlock with borrowers. Suppliers interlock with customers. Competitors sometimes interlock (though this raises legal issues).

üìñ Empirical Pattern: Banks and Borrowers

Research consistently shows that:

  • Companies with high debt are more likely to have bankers on their boards
  • After a financing crisis, bank interlocks increase
  • Once debt is paid down, bank interlocks often dissolve

This supports RDT: interlocks form to manage resource dependencies, not randomly.

When Strategies Fail

Not all dependency management succeeds. Common failure modes include:

  • Misreading Dependencies: Focusing on the wrong environmental actors
  • Overreach: Acquisition of unrelated businesses that create new problems
  • Counter-Mobilization: Powerful actors resist co-optation attempts
  • Regulatory Backlash: Political action invites scrutiny and new regulations
  • Lock-In: Strategies that worked become rigid constraints
