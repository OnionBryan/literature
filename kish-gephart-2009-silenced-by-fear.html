Silenced by Fear:
The Nature, Sources, and Consequences of Fear at Work

Kish-Gephart, J. J., Detert, J. R., Treviño, L. K., & Edmondson, A. C. (2009)
Research in Organizational Behavior, 29, 163-193

"Even in a work environment of psychological safety, where one generally feels safe speaking up, the deep-rooted fear of challenging authority is not erased."

Executive Summary

In every organization, employees have the potential to speak up about important issues—yet they often remain silent out of fear. This comprehensive review develops an integrative model of workplace fear, drawing on evolutionary psychology, neuroscience, sociology, and organizational behavior to explain why fear is such a powerful and persistent barrier to voice.

Central Argument: Fear is a discrete emotion with evolutionary roots that continues to shape workplace behavior. Even in psychologically safe environments, deeply ingrained fear responses can override rational assessments of risk, causing employees to remain silent when they should speak up.

The Nature of Fear: An Evolutionary Perspective

Fear as Adaptive Response

The predisposition to learn to fear particular stimuli is central to human survival. Fear evolved as a mechanism to protect us from threats—and the brain's fear circuits don't distinguish between physical dangers and social threats like criticism from authority figures.

Automatic Fear Response

Fear triggers automatic physiological responses (increased heart rate, cortisol release) before conscious thought occurs. This means employees can feel afraid before they consciously evaluate whether fear is warranted.

Learned Fear

Learning provides the opportunity for individuals to respond automatically to perceived danger without needing to continuously re-learn which stimuli are dangerous. One bad experience with authority can create lasting fear.

Fear Generalization

Fear learned in one context (childhood, school, previous jobs) generalizes to new contexts. Employees bring their accumulated fear experiences to every new workplace.

Resistance to Extinction

Unlike positive emotions, fear is remarkably resistant to change. A single bad experience can re-trigger fear responses that had been dormant for years.

The Neuroscience of Fear

The amygdala, the brain's fear center, processes threat information faster than the cortex can engage in conscious reasoning. This means:

  • Speed over accuracy: The brain prioritizes fast threat detection over careful analysis
  • False positives are costly but survivable: Being afraid of something harmless wastes energy but keeps you alive
  • False negatives can be fatal: Failing to fear a real threat can kill you—so the brain errs on the side of fear
  • Social threats activate same circuits: Criticism from a boss activates the same fear circuits as physical danger

Three Sources of Workplace Fear

1. Dispositional Sources (Individual Differences)

Some individuals are more prone to fear due to personality traits, prior experiences, or neurobiological factors. High trait anxiety, low self-esteem, and histories of punishment for speaking up create a predisposition toward workplace fear. These factors travel with the employee across jobs and contexts.

2. Contextual Sources (Work Environment)

Organizational hierarchy creates inherent power asymmetries. Bosses control rewards, punishments, and career outcomes. Even well-intentioned leaders create fear simply by occupying positions of power. Organizational cultures that punish messengers amplify this effect. Observing colleagues being punished for speaking up creates vicarious fear.

3. Evolutionary Sources (Human Nature)

Humans evolved in hierarchical groups where challenging dominant individuals carried real risks. Deference to authority was adaptive—and those instincts remain. We are biologically primed to be cautious around those with power over us, regardless of their actual intentions or behavior.

Types of Fear-Based Silence

The authors identify different forms of silence that result from workplace fear:

Acquiescent Silence

Passive withholding based on resignation and feeling unable to make a difference.

"Why bother? Nothing will change anyway."

Defensive Silence

Active withholding to protect oneself from punishment or negative consequences.

"Speaking up will hurt my career."

ProSocial Silence

Withholding information to protect others or the organization.

"Raising this will embarrass my colleague."

Diffident Silence

Withholding due to lack of confidence in one's ideas.

"My opinion probably isn't valuable enough to share."

Key Insights

Psychological Safety Is Necessary But Not Sufficient

"Even in a work environment of psychological safety, where one generally feels safe speaking up, the deep-rooted fear of challenging authority is not erased. A single bad experience with authority—direct or indirect—will likely re-trigger the automatic fear response."

The Pervasiveness of Fear-Based Silence

Despite being identified as a pervasive emotion in the modern workplace, fear has not received corresponding attention from management researchers. This paper argues that fear deserves central attention in theories of organizational behavior because:

  • It operates at multiple levels (individual, group, organization)
  • It has deep evolutionary and neurobiological roots
  • It is remarkably resistant to change
  • It affects behavior even when consciously overridden
  • It compounds over time through learned associations

The Context of Organizational Hierarchy

Individuals make voice decisions within the context of organizational hierarchy, where "bosses who do not wish to hear about problems can punish people for speaking up" and within "a social system that has implicit norms about the desirability of speaking up." This dual context—formal hierarchy plus informal norms—creates a powerful silencing force.

The Integrative Model of Fear and Silence

Understanding how fear sources interact to produce workplace silence

The Multi-Level Model

How the Sources Interact

The three sources of fear don't operate independently—they interact and amplify each other:

Interaction Mechanism Example
Dispositional × Contextual Individual differences determine sensitivity to context High-anxiety individuals are more affected by punitive cultures
Contextual × Evolutionary Organizational hierarchy activates evolved deference instincts Formal authority triggers automatic submission responses
Dispositional × Evolutionary Individual learning history shapes evolutionary fear expression Past punishment for challenging authority strengthens deference

The Fear Re-Triggering Cycle

One of the paper's key insights is how easily fear can be re-triggered:

  1. Initial learning: Employee experiences punishment for speaking up (directly or vicariously)
  2. Fear conditioning: Brain associates speaking up with threat
  3. Apparent extinction: In safe environment, fear seems to fade
  4. Re-triggering: Single negative experience reactivates full fear response
  5. Generalization: Fear spreads to new contexts and authority figures

Practical Implications

What organizations, leaders, and individuals can do about fear-based silence

For Organizations

Accept That Fear Cannot Be Eliminated

Because fear has evolutionary roots and is remarkably resistant to change, organizations should not aim to eliminate fear entirely. Instead, they should:

Realistic Fear Management Strategies

  • + Create conditions where fear is minimized (though never eliminated)
  • + Provide alternative channels for voice that feel safer (anonymous systems, ombudspersons)
  • + Acknowledge that even supportive environments trigger fear responses
  • + Design systems that compensate for fear-based silence (proactive inquiry, structured input)
  • + Train managers to actively solicit input rather than waiting for it

Address All Three Sources

Dispositional Interventions

Consider fear-proneness in selection. Provide coaching and support for anxious employees. Create psychological safety training that addresses individual fears.

Contextual Interventions

Train leaders to respond positively to voice. Publicly reward those who speak up. Protect messengers. Remove punitive elements from culture.

Evolutionary Interventions

Educate employees about evolutionary fear responses. Normalize fear as natural, not shameful. Create structures that reduce hierarchy salience.

For Leaders

Recognize Your Inherent Fear-Inducing Power

Even well-intentioned, supportive leaders create fear simply by occupying positions of authority. The evolutionary response to hierarchy is automatic—employees will feel some fear of you regardless of your behavior.

Leader Action Checklist

  • + Assume fear exists: Don't wait for employees to come to you—proactively ask for concerns
  • + Respond positively consistently: One negative reaction can undo months of positive ones
  • + Thank messengers publicly: Make it visibly safe to bring bad news
  • + Model vulnerability: Admit your own mistakes and uncertainties
  • + Watch for re-triggering: After any negative event, actively rebuild safety
  • + Create structural safety: Use skip-levels, anonymous surveys, and ombudspersons

The 10:1 Recovery Ratio

Research suggests it takes approximately 10 positive experiences to overcome 1 negative experience when it comes to fear conditioning. This means that a single punitive response to employee voice can require months of consistent positive behavior to repair. Leaders must be vigilant about every interaction.

Watch for Re-Triggering Events

Be especially vigilant after any event that might re-trigger fear: organizational changes, layoffs, public criticism of an employee, or any punishment that might be seen as connected to speaking up.

For Individuals

Understand Your Fear Is Normal

Fear of authority is not a personal weakness—it's a product of human evolution and your accumulated experiences. Understanding this can reduce shame and enable more conscious decision-making about when to speak up.

Personal Strategies for Managing Fear

  • + Name the fear: Acknowledge "I'm feeling afraid" rather than avoiding the emotion
  • + Distinguish sources: Is this dispositional, contextual, or evolutionary fear?
  • + Reality-test: What's the actual probability of negative consequences?
  • + Start small: Build voice confidence with low-stakes issues first
  • + Find allies: Speak up with supporters present when possible
  • + Prepare thoroughly: Reduce anxiety through preparation and practice
  • + Track outcomes: Keep a record of voice attempts and results to build evidence

Distinguish Automatic Fear from Rational Risk Assessment

When you feel afraid to speak up, ask: "Is this fear based on real current threats, or is it an automatic response triggered by past experiences or evolutionary instincts?" This conscious questioning can help override inappropriate fear responses.

Build Evidence Against Your Fears

Systematically test your fears by speaking up in low-stakes situations. Track the outcomes. Over time, you may build evidence that your fear is overstated in your current context—though be prepared for occasional setbacks that may re-trigger fear.

The Voice Experiment: Choose one low-stakes issue this week where you would normally stay silent. Speak up. Note the outcome. Repeat. Over time, you'll build a personal database of evidence about what actually happens when you use your voice—evidence that can compete with your fear-based predictions.
