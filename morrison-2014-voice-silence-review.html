Employee Voice and Silence:
Taking Stock a Decade Later

Morrison, E. W. (2014)
Annual Review of Organizational Psychology and Organizational Behavior, 1, 173-197

A comprehensive synthesis of research on when and why employees choose to speak up or remain silent

Executive Summary

Elizabeth Morrison's authoritative review synthesizes over a decade of research on employee voice and silence, providing the field's most comprehensive framework for understanding these critical workplace behaviors. The review organizes hundreds of studies around key antecedents and consequences, identifying what we know and—importantly—what we still don't understand.

Defining Voice and Silence:
  • Voice: Discretionary communication of ideas, suggestions, concerns, or opinions about work-related issues with the intent to improve organizational or unit functioning
  • Silence: The intentional withholding of such communication

Key Distinctions: Types of Voice

Morrison distinguishes between different types of voice, which have different antecedents and consequences:

Promotive Voice

Expressing new ideas, suggestions for improvement, or recommendations for positive changes. Forward-looking and focused on opportunities. Generally perceived more positively by recipients.

Prohibitive Voice

Speaking up about problems, concerns, or potential threats. Warning about things that are going wrong. More risky because it implies criticism and may challenge the status quo.

The Voice Decision Process

Morrison conceptualizes voice as a decision process involving several psychological states:

Three Critical Psychological States

1. Perceived Efficacy
"Will speaking up make a difference?"
If no → Silence
2. Perceived Safety
"Will speaking up be risky for me?"
If yes → Silence
3. Felt Obligation
"Is it my responsibility to speak up?"
If no → Silence

The Calculation

Employees weigh the potential benefits of voice (efficacy × value of change) against the potential costs (risk × likelihood of negative outcomes). This calculation is influenced by individual factors, contextual factors, and the characteristics of the issue itself.

Contextual Antecedents: The Role of Leadership

The review highlights leadership as perhaps the most important contextual factor affecting voice:

Psychological Safety

Edmondson's (1999) construct remains central. Teams with high psychological safety see more voice. Leaders create safety through inclusive behavior and positive responses to voice.

Leader Openness

Employees assess whether their specific leader is open to input. This is distinct from general psychological safety and has independent effects on voice.

Transformational Leadership

Transformational leaders encourage voice by intellectually stimulating followers, expressing confidence in them, and articulating a compelling vision that makes employees want to contribute.

Leader-Member Exchange (LMX)

High-quality exchange relationships provide the trust and obligation that facilitate voice. Employees in high LMX relationships feel safer speaking up and more obligated to do so.

Key Finding: Leadership factors consistently emerge as among the strongest predictors of employee voice. The immediate supervisor has disproportionate influence on whether employees feel safe and motivated to speak up.

Critical Gaps Identified

Morrison highlights several important gaps in the research that require future attention:

1. Consequences of Voice (and Silence)

Most research focuses on antecedents. We know less about what happens after voice: When does it lead to change? When does it backfire? What are the long-term career consequences? How does silence accumulate into organizational problems?

2. The Target's Perspective

Research is heavily speaker-focused. We need more understanding of how recipients process and respond to voice, what makes them receptive vs. defensive, and how they decide whether to act on input.

3. Collective Silence

Most research examines individual voice decisions. Yet silence is often collective—whole teams or organizations where "nobody talks about" certain issues. The dynamics of collective silence are understudied.

4. The Content of Voice

Research typically treats voice as a single behavior. But the specific content (what is said, how it's framed) likely matters greatly for outcomes. More attention needed to message characteristics.

Theoretical Integration

Morrison integrates multiple theoretical perspectives on voice:

Theory Contribution to Understanding Voice
Exit-Voice-Loyalty-Neglect Voice as one response to dissatisfaction (vs. leaving, staying loyal, or disengaging)
Social Exchange Theory Voice as discretionary behavior driven by reciprocity and obligation
Expectancy Theory Voice as motivated by expected outcomes and perceived efficacy
Planned Behavior Theory Voice as intentional behavior shaped by attitudes, norms, and perceived control
Psychological Safety Voice as enabled by perceived interpersonal risk-taking safety

Antecedents of Voice and Silence

What determines whether employees speak up or stay silent?

Complete Antecedent Framework

Morrison organizes antecedents into four major categories:

1. Individual Dispositions

  • Proactive personality: General tendency to take initiative
  • Extraversion: Comfort with social interaction
  • Conscientiousness: Sense of duty and responsibility
  • Self-efficacy: Confidence in one's communication abilities
  • Locus of control: Belief in ability to influence outcomes
  • Implicit voice theories: Deep beliefs about appropriateness of speaking up

2. Job Attitudes

  • Job satisfaction: Mixed effects—may increase promotive voice but dissatisfaction may drive prohibitive voice
  • Organizational commitment: Creates felt obligation to improve the organization
  • Organizational identification: "We" orientation increases willingness to contribute
  • Psychological ownership: Feeling of investment increases voice

3. Leadership and Supervision

  • Leader openness: Perceived receptivity to input
  • Transformational leadership: Inspirational, supportive leader behavior
  • Leader-member exchange (LMX): Quality of dyadic relationship
  • Abusive supervision: Strong negative effect on voice
  • Ethical leadership: Creates climate supportive of voice

4. Organizational Context

  • Psychological safety: Team-level perception that voice is safe
  • Voice climate: Shared perception that voice is encouraged
  • Formal voice mechanisms: Suggestion systems, forums, etc.
  • Organizational structure: Hierarchy, formalization, centralization
  • Group norms: Shared expectations about appropriate behavior

The Mediating Psychological States

Antecedents affect voice through three mediating psychological states:

Perceived Efficacy of Voice

"Will my input make a difference?" Employees must believe that speaking up will lead to meaningful change. Without efficacy beliefs, even safe environments won't generate voice.

Perceived Safety of Voice

"Is it safe for me to speak up?" Employees assess potential negative consequences—embarrassment, damaged relationships, career penalties. Safety concerns can override efficacy beliefs.

Felt Obligation for Voice

"Is it my responsibility to speak up?" Some employees feel duty-bound to voice concerns; others see it as optional. Obligation can motivate voice even when efficacy or safety is uncertain.

Consequences of Voice and Silence

What happens when employees speak up—or stay silent?

Consequences at Three Levels

Individual Consequences

Potential Positive Outcomes
  • Recognition and appreciation
  • Enhanced reputation as contributor
  • Career advancement
  • Sense of meaning and engagement
Potential Negative Outcomes
  • Being labeled as troublemaker
  • Damaged relationships
  • Retaliation or career penalties
  • Social exclusion

Team-Level Consequences

Of Voice:

  • Improved decision-making through diverse input
  • Earlier detection of problems and errors
  • Enhanced team learning and adaptation
  • Stronger collective problem-solving

Of Silence:

  • Undetected errors and problems
  • Reduced learning from mistakes
  • Groupthink and premature consensus
  • Erosion of trust and psychological safety

Organizational Consequences

Of Voice:

  • Innovation through bottom-up ideas
  • Improved operational efficiency
  • Better strategic decisions
  • Organizational learning and adaptation

Of Silence:

  • Strategic blind spots
  • Failure to detect and correct problems
  • Reduced innovation
  • Organizational disasters from unvoiced concerns

The Paradox of Voice Consequences

The Fundamental Tension: Voice can have both positive and negative consequences for the individual speaker—and the outcome is uncertain. This uncertainty is itself a barrier to voice, as risk-averse employees choose the certain outcome of silence over the uncertain outcome of speaking up.

Research suggests that consequences depend heavily on:

  • How voice is delivered: Constructive framing, appropriate timing, and respectful delivery improve outcomes
  • Who delivers it: High performers and well-liked employees experience better outcomes
  • Who receives it: Leader characteristics determine response
  • Organizational context: Voice-supportive cultures buffer negative consequences
  • Content of voice: Promotive voice typically received better than prohibitive voice
