A Garbage Can Model of Organizational Choice

Michael D. Cohen, James G. March, & Johan P. Olsen

Administrative Science Quarterly, 1972

Abstract

Cohen, March, and Olsen present a radical alternative to rational models of organizational decision-making. In "organized anarchies" like universities, decisions are not made through systematic problem-solving. Instead, four relatively independent streams‚Äîproblems, solutions, participants, and choice opportunities‚Äîflow through the organization. A decision occurs when these streams happen to intersect at a "garbage can" (choice opportunity). This model reveals that power often belongs not to those with the best ideas, but to those with the persistence, time, and energy to be present when opportunities arise.

Overview: Decision-Making in Organized Anarchies

The "garbage can" model emerged from James March's work at the Carnegie School, which had already challenged classical assumptions about organizational rationality. But this paper went further, suggesting that in certain organizational contexts, decision-making is fundamentally decoupled from rational problem-solving.

The Organized Anarchy

The model applies specifically to "organized anarchies"‚Äîorganizations characterized by:

  • Problematic Preferences: Goals are unclear, inconsistent, or discovered through action rather than guiding it
  • Unclear Technology: Members don't fully understand the organization's own processes; they learn by trial and error
  • Fluid Participation: Participants wander in and out of decision arenas; attention is scarce and variable

Universities are the paradigmatic example, but elements of organized anarchy exist in many organizations‚Äîespecially in strategic planning, R&D, and any context characterized by ambiguity and professional autonomy.

The Four Streams

Rather than a linear process of "identify problem ‚Üí generate solutions ‚Üí choose best option," the garbage can model posits four independent streams flowing through the organization:

Problems

Issues and concerns that people bring to the organization. Problems exist independently of solutions‚Äîpeople may have concerns without knowing how to address them, or solutions without knowing what problems they solve.

Solutions

Answers looking for questions. People often have preferred solutions (technologies, approaches, ideas) that they carry around and attach to whatever problems become available. "A solution is an answer actively looking for a question."

Participants

Decision-makers with varying amounts of time and energy. Attention is the scarce resource. Who happens to be in the room when a decision is made profoundly shapes the outcome.

Choice Opportunities

Occasions when the organization is expected to produce a decision‚Äîmeetings, deadlines, scheduled reviews. These are the "garbage cans" into which the other streams are dumped.

When Decisions Happen

A decision occurs when these four streams happen to connect at a choice opportunity. The resulting decision may have little to do with rational problem-solving‚Äîit depends on what problems, solutions, and participants happen to be present when the garbage can is "opened."

The Garbage Can Model in Detail

Three Decision Styles

The model identifies three ways decisions can be made, only one of which resembles rational choice:

1. Resolution

The rational ideal: a choice is made by resolving problems. A problem enters a garbage can, an appropriate solution is found, and the problem is solved. This is the rarest outcome in the model.

2. Oversight

A choice is made quickly before problems arrive. If a choice opportunity becomes available and participants make a decision before problems attach to it, the decision can be made by "oversight"‚Äîessentially, a decision without deliberation.

3. Flight

Problems leave to attach to other choice opportunities. A problem may enter a garbage can but then "fly away" to a more attractive opportunity, leaving the original choice to be made without it. The problem isn't solved‚Äîit just moves elsewhere.

Key Dynamics

Energy Load and Decision Speed

When participants have limited energy and many choice opportunities compete for attention, decisions are more likely to be made by oversight or flight. Heavy loads prevent thorough deliberation.

Access Structure

Not all problems and solutions have equal access to all choice opportunities. Formal rules, informal norms, and political dynamics determine what can be discussed where. Restricting access can prevent problems from overwhelming certain garbage cans.

Decision Structure

Not all participants have equal access to all choice opportunities. Some people are always in the room; others rarely. This creates systematic advantages for those with time and access.

Implications for Organizational Behavior

The garbage can model suggests several counter-intuitive organizational phenomena:

  • Important problems may never get solved because they keep flying from garbage can to garbage can
  • Solutions may be implemented without a clear understanding of what problem they solve
  • The same proposal may succeed or fail depending on what else is in the garbage can at the time
  • Increasing the number of choice opportunities may slow decision-making by dispersing attention
  • Sometimes the best way to make a decision is to do it quickly, before it attracts problems

Power & Strategic Implications

Power in the Garbage Can

The garbage can model reveals a distinctive form of power: not the power to control outcomes directly, but the power to shape what enters which garbage cans and when.

Sources of Power in Organized Anarchies

  • Persistence: The power to keep showing up, to wait out others, to still be there when the decision is finally made
  • Time and Energy: Those who can devote attention to a decision have disproportionate influence
  • Agenda Control: The power to determine which problems and solutions enter which garbage cans
  • Timing: Knowing when to introduce proposals‚Äîbefore problems accumulate, or when key opponents are absent
  • Problem Attachment: The ability to attach or detach problems from choice opportunities

Strategic Implications

For Those Seeking Change
  • Have your solution ready when problems emerge
  • Attach your solution to high-priority problems
  • Be present at as many choice opportunities as possible
  • Time proposals for moments of crisis or opportunity
For Those Blocking Change
  • Overload choice opportunities with problems
  • Introduce competing solutions to create confusion
  • Divert problems to different garbage cans
  • Ensure your presence at key decisions

The Persistence Advantage

Perhaps the most profound implication: in organized anarchies, power belongs to those who persist. Ideas don't win because they're best‚Äîthey win because their champions keep showing up. This creates structural advantages for:

  • Senior members with accumulated political capital and routinized access
  • Full-time administrators over part-time faculty or board members
  • Those without competing time demands (favoring those with fewer external obligations)
  • Those who enjoy the process (political actors who find meetings energizing, not draining)

Beyond Rationality

The garbage can model doesn't suggest that rationality is impossible‚Äîbut that it's one decision style among several, and often not the dominant one. Recognizing this opens space for alternative strategies that work with, rather than against, organizational ambiguity.
