The Small World Problem

Stanley Milgram

Psychology Today, 1967

üîó Six Degrees of Separation

Abstract

Milgram's landmark experiment demonstrated the surprising interconnectedness of human social networks. By asking participants to forward letters to a target person through personal acquaintances, he discovered that people are connected by remarkably short chains‚Äîon average just six intermediaries. This "small world" phenomenon revealed that social networks, while seemingly vast, are efficiently structured for information flow. The study laid groundwork for understanding how resources, opportunities, and influence travel through networks, showing that the path to any person‚Äîincluding powerful ones‚Äîis often shorter than we imagine.

Overview: A Surprisingly Connected World

In the 1960s, Stanley Milgram posed a simple but profound question: How many intermediary acquaintances does it take to connect any two randomly selected people in the United States? The answer‚Äîroughly six‚Äîchallenged intuitions about the vastness of human society and launched an entirely new field of network science.

üí° The Counterintuitive Finding

Despite the U.S. population of 200 million, any two people were connected through just a handful of acquaintances. The world is both vast and intimately connected‚Äî"small" in a structural sense even when geographically enormous.

Why "Small World" Matters

The small world phenomenon has profound implications beyond social curiosity:

  • Information Spread: News, rumors, and innovations can travel remarkably fast through social networks
  • Disease Transmission: Epidemics spread through the same network structures
  • Job Finding: Granovetter later showed that most jobs come through network connections
  • Access to Power: Influential people are often closer than we think‚Äîa few introductions away
  • Social Capital: Your network position determines what information and opportunities reach you

The Experiment: Tracing Human Chains

The Experimental Design

Milgram's methodology was elegantly simple:

Experimental Procedure

  1. Select a "target person"‚Äîa stockbroker in Boston
  2. Recruit starting participants from Omaha, Nebraska and Wichita, Kansas
  3. Give each starter a document with the target's name, occupation, and location
  4. Instruction: Send to someone you know on a first-name basis who might be closer to the target
  5. Each recipient records their name and forwards the chain
  6. Track how many steps until the document reaches the target

Key Findings

üìä Chain Length Distribution

Of the 64 chains that reached the target:

  • Shortest chain: 2 intermediaries
  • Longest chain: 11 intermediaries
  • Median chain length: 5 people
  • Most common: 5-6 intermediaries
üîë The "Funneling" Effect

A remarkable pattern emerged: many chains converged through the same few people near the target. One clothing merchant alone accounted for over 25% of successful chains. This revealed the existence of highly connected "hubs" or "connectors" in social networks‚Äîpeople who bridge otherwise disconnected social worlds.

Limitations and Criticisms

The study had important limitations:

  • Completion Rate: Only 64 of 296 chains (22%) reached the target‚Äîthose that failed might have been much longer
  • Self-Selection: People who forwarded chains may be more connected than average
  • Strategy vs. Reality: Participants chose routes they thought would work‚Äîactual social distance might differ
  • U.S. Specific: Results might not generalize to other societies or cultures

Despite these limitations, subsequent studies‚Äîincluding a 2003 email-based replication with 60,000+ participants‚Äîconfirmed the basic finding: most people are connected through 5-7 intermediaries.

Network Implications: Power and Access

The Network Structure Behind Small Worlds

Later research (especially Watts & Strogatz, 1998) revealed why small worlds exist. The key ingredients:

Small World Network Structure

  • High Clustering: Your friends likely know each other (local clusters)
  • Short Path Lengths: Despite clustering, any two people are connected by few steps
  • The Secret: A few "long-range ties" that bridge distant clusters dramatically shorten paths

Implications for Power and Opportunity

üíº Job Market Access

Granovetter's "Strength of Weak Ties" (1973) showed that most jobs come through acquaintances, not close friends. In a small world, your weak ties connect you to information and opportunities circulating in distant clusters. Those without diverse networks are cut off from these flows.

üëî Access to Decision-Makers

The small world phenomenon suggests that powerful people are often just a few introductions away. But here's the catch: knowing the path exists doesn't mean you can travel it. Each link requires someone willing to make an introduction‚Äîand willingness depends on the strength and nature of the relationship.

üì¢ Information and Influence Spread

In small world networks, information (and misinformation) can spread rapidly. Ideas, trends, and innovations that catch on can reach most of the population in just a few "generations" of transmission. This has implications for viral marketing, social movements, and public health.

The Unequal Small World

While the world may be structurally small, it's not equally small for everyone:

  • Hub Positions: Those who bridge multiple social worlds (connectors) have shorter paths to everyone
  • Peripheral Positions: Those embedded in only one cluster have longer paths and less access
  • Social Barriers: Class, race, geography create clusters that are hard to bridge
  • Reciprocity Gap: You may know someone is 3 degrees away, but they may not accept the connection

üîç Strategic Implication

Understanding small world structure suggests a strategy: cultivate diverse ties across different social worlds. One "bridging" relationship to a new cluster can dramatically expand your effective network reach.
