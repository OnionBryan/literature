An Exploratory Study of Employee Silence

Milliken, F. J., Morrison, E. W., & Hewlin, P. F. (2003)
Journal of Management Studies, 40(6), 1453-1476

"Issues That Employees Don't Communicate Upward and Why"

Executive Summary

This foundational qualitative study provided the first systematic empirical examination of employee silence—what employees choose not to communicate upward to their superiors and why. Through in-depth interviews with 40 full-time employees across diverse industries, the researchers identified patterns in the topics employees avoid and the reasons behind their self-censorship.

Key Finding: Employee silence is widespread and deliberate. Approximately 85% of respondents reported that on at least one occasion they felt unable to raise an issue or concern to their bosses. The most common reason: fear of being labeled negatively ("troublemaker," "complainer," "not a team player").

Research Design

Methodology

The researchers conducted semi-structured interviews with 40 MBA students who were working full-time in various industries. The qualitative approach was chosen because silence was an understudied phenomenon requiring exploratory, inductive research to identify its key dimensions.

Sample Characteristics

  • Industries: Consulting, financial services, consumer products, media, pharmaceuticals, telecommunications, and others
  • Experience: Ranged from 3 to 20+ years of work experience
  • Levels: Mix of junior and mid-level employees
  • Gender: Approximately equal representation

Interview Protocol

Each interview lasted approximately 45 minutes and asked employees to describe:

  1. Instances when they did not raise an issue or concern with their manager
  2. The specific issue they chose not to raise
  3. Their reasons for remaining silent
  4. The consequences (perceived or actual) of speaking up in their organization

What Employees Don't Discuss: Five Categories

1. Concerns About Colleague or Supervisor Performance/Competence

The most frequently mentioned category. Employees remained silent about:

  • A colleague's poor work quality or productivity
  • A supervisor's questionable decisions or competence
  • Team members not pulling their weight
  • Someone taking credit for others' work

2. Organizational Processes and Procedures

Employees avoided raising concerns about:

  • Inefficient work processes
  • Unclear roles and responsibilities
  • Poor coordination between departments
  • Bureaucratic obstacles to getting work done

3. Compensation and Benefits

Employees hesitated to discuss:

  • Perceived pay inequities
  • Dissatisfaction with compensation
  • Benefits concerns
  • Promotion decisions

4. Disagreement with Company Policies or Decisions

Employees stayed silent about:

  • Strategic decisions they disagreed with
  • Policies they found unfair or counterproductive
  • Changes they thought were misguided
  • Organizational priorities they questioned

5. Ethical or Legal Issues

Perhaps most concerning, employees sometimes stayed silent about:

  • Ethical concerns about business practices
  • Potential legal violations
  • Harassment or discrimination
  • Safety concerns

Why Employees Stay Silent: Four Reasons

1. Fear of Being Labeled Negatively

The most common reason. Employees feared being seen as "troublemakers," "complainers," "not team players," or "negative." This labeling concern was pervasive across industries and levels.

2. Fear of Damaged Relationships

Employees worried that raising concerns would harm their relationships with supervisors or colleagues. The interpersonal costs of voice were seen as significant.

3. Fear of Retaliation or Punishment

Employees feared tangible negative consequences: being passed over for promotion, receiving poor performance reviews, losing their jobs, or being assigned undesirable work.

4. Belief That Speaking Up Wouldn't Matter

Many employees stayed silent because they believed their input wouldn't be heard or wouldn't lead to change. Why take the risk if nothing will come of it?

Theoretical Contributions

Establishing Silence as a Phenomenon

Prior to this study, most research focused on "voice"—when employees speak up. This paper established "silence" as a distinct phenomenon worthy of study in its own right. Silence is not simply the absence of voice; it is an active, intentional choice to withhold information.

"Silence is not merely the absence of voice. It is a deliberate, conscious decision to withhold ideas, information, or concerns that could be valuable to the organization."

The Social Construction of "Undiscussables"

The research revealed that organizations develop implicit norms about what topics are "discussable" and which are not. These norms are rarely explicit but are learned through observation and experience. New employees quickly learn what can and cannot be raised.

The Individual Decision Calculus

The study showed that silence results from an implicit cost-benefit calculation: employees weigh the potential benefits of speaking up against the potential costs. When costs are perceived to outweigh benefits—even if the perception is inaccurate—silence results.

Key Findings in Depth

What the interviews revealed about organizational silence

The Pervasiveness of Silence

Finding 1: Silence is Widespread

Approximately 85% of respondents reported at least one occasion when they felt unable to raise a concern with their boss. This was not occasional or exceptional behavior—it was a routine part of organizational life across industries, levels, and company sizes.

Finding 2: Silence Often Involves Important Issues

Employees weren't just staying silent about trivial matters. Many reported withholding information about ethical concerns, serious performance problems, and strategic missteps—issues with significant organizational consequences.

Finding 3: Silence is Deliberate

When asked about their silence, employees described a conscious decision process. They weighed options, considered consequences, and chose not to speak. This was not passive—it was active self-censorship.

The Psychology of Self-Censorship

Identity Concerns Dominate

The single most powerful driver of silence was concern about how speaking up would affect others' perceptions. Employees were not primarily afraid of tangible punishment—they were afraid of being seen differently: as negative, as troublemakers, as not team players.

Key Insight: The fear of negative labeling is particularly insidious because it operates even when leaders are genuinely receptive. Employees' concern is not just "Will my boss punish me?" but "Will my boss (and others) see me as someone who complains?"

The Relationship Preservation Motive

Many employees expressed strong desire to maintain positive relationships with supervisors and colleagues. They viewed raising concerns—especially about people's performance or behavior—as inherently threatening to relationships, regardless of how constructively the concern was framed.

Learned Futility

Some employees had learned through experience (their own or others') that speaking up didn't lead to change. This "learned futility" created a self-fulfilling prophecy: employees don't speak up because nothing will change, and nothing changes because employees don't speak up.

Organizational and Contextual Factors

Hierarchical Position Matters

Lower-level employees reported more silence than those at higher levels. The power differential between employee and supervisor was a consistent factor in the decision to remain silent.

Observation of Others' Experiences

Employees learned about the risks of voice not just from their own experiences but from watching what happened to others who spoke up. A single instance of someone being punished for voice could create widespread silence.

Industry and Culture Variation

While silence was present across all industries studied, its prevalence and the specific topics avoided varied by organizational culture. Some organizations had stronger norms against raising certain issues (e.g., compensation) than others.

Practical Implications

Breaking the silence in organizations

For Organizations

Recognize That Silence Exists

The first step is acknowledging that employees are withholding valuable information. Leaders who believe they have "open" cultures may be unaware of the silence happening below the surface. The 85% finding should give every leader pause.

Identify Your Organization's "Undiscussables"

Every organization has implicit norms about what can and cannot be discussed. Leaders should actively try to surface these norms:

  • Conduct confidential surveys asking about topics employees avoid
  • Use external consultants to conduct frank interviews
  • Create safe spaces for anonymous feedback
  • Ask departing employees about what they didn't say while employed

Address the Root Causes

Counter Labeling Fears

Publicly celebrate and reward those who raise concerns constructively. Make "speaking up" a valued identity, not a stigmatized one.

Protect Relationships

Train employees and managers in how to raise and receive concerns in ways that preserve relationships. Normalize constructive disagreement.

Prevent Retaliation

Create clear protections for those who speak up. Investigate and punish any retaliation. Make consequences visible.

Demonstrate Responsiveness

When employees do speak up, respond visibly. Show that voice leads to change. Break the cycle of learned futility.

For Leaders

You Are Part of the Problem

As a leader, you represent authority—and authority triggers silence. Even if you're genuinely open, employees will be hesitant to raise concerns with you. You must actively counteract this dynamic.

Specific Leader Behaviors

  • Explicitly invite dissent: "I want to hear what might be wrong with this idea."
  • Respond non-defensively: Thank people for concerns, even unwelcome ones.
  • Follow up: Get back to people about how their concerns were addressed.
  • Model vulnerability: Admit your own mistakes and uncertainties.
  • Watch for silence: In meetings, notice who isn't speaking. Follow up privately.

For Individuals

Understand Your Own Silence

Reflect on what you don't say at work and why. Are your reasons based on accurate assessments of risk, or on assumptions that may be outdated or exaggerated?

Test Your Assumptions

Consider raising lower-stakes concerns first to test whether your fears are warranted. Build evidence about how voice is actually received in your specific context.

Frame Voice Constructively

How you raise concerns matters. Research suggests that voice framed around organizational benefit (rather than personal complaint) is received more positively. Focus on problems and solutions, not blame.

Find Allies

Collective voice is often safer and more effective than individual voice. If you have concerns, chances are others do too. Finding allies can make speaking up feel less risky.

The Cost of Silence

What Organizations Lose to Silence:
  • Early warning of problems before they become crises
  • Ideas for innovation and improvement
  • Feedback on strategic decisions
  • Detection of ethical violations
  • Employee engagement and commitment
  • Organizational learning and adaptation

The researchers argue that employee silence represents a significant organizational dysfunction—one that is often invisible to leaders because, by definition, they don't hear what employees aren't saying. This "organizational silence" can perpetuate problems, stifle innovation, and ultimately harm organizational performance.
