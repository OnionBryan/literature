An Approach-Inhibition Model of Employee Silence

Morrison, E. W., See, K. E., & Pan, C. (2015)
Personnel Psychology, 68(3), 547-580

How personal sense of power and perceived leader openness jointly determine whether employees speak up or stay silent

Executive Summary

This research applies Dacher Keltner's influential approach-inhibition theory of power to explain employee silence. The key insight: employees' personal sense of power—their subjective feeling of having influence—interacts with their perceptions of leader openness to determine whether they speak up or remain silent.

Central Finding: Employees with a low personal sense of power are especially likely to remain silent, but this effect is buffered when they perceive their leader as open to input. The combination of low power + perceived leader openness can enable voice that would otherwise be inhibited.

The Neural Systems Behind Voice and Silence

Behavioral Approach System (BAS) vs. Behavioral Inhibition System (BIS)

The Approach-Inhibition Theory of Power

Keltner, Gruenfeld, and Anderson's (2003) approach-inhibition theory provides the theoretical foundation. The theory is grounded in neuroscience research on the Behavioral Approach System (BAS) and Behavioral Inhibition System (BIS):

Behavioral Approach System (BAS)

Activated by reward cues and opportunities. Produces positive affect, attention to rewards, automatic/confident processing, and approach behaviors. High power activates BAS.

Behavioral Inhibition System (BIS)

Activated by threat cues and punishment. Produces negative affect, attention to threats, controlled/cautious processing, and inhibition behaviors. Low power activates BIS.

Dimension High Power (BAS Activated) Low Power (BIS Activated)
Affect Positive emotions, confidence, optimism Negative emotions, anxiety, vigilance
Attention Focus on rewards and opportunities Focus on threats and punishments
Cognition Automatic, heuristic processing Controlled, systematic processing
Behavior Disinhibited, assertive, risk-taking Inhibited, cautious, risk-averse
Voice Likelihood Higher—approach orientation Lower—avoidance orientation

Personal Sense of Power: The Key Variable

What Is Personal Sense of Power?

Personal sense of power is a psychological state—the individual's subjective perception of their ability to influence others and control resources. It is distinct from:

  • Objective power: Actual position in hierarchy or control over resources
  • Power motivation: Desire to have power over others
  • Self-efficacy: General confidence in one's abilities
Why Personal Sense of Power Matters: Two employees with the same objective position can have very different personal sense of power. The subjective experience—not the objective reality—is what activates BAS or BIS and determines behavioral tendencies.

Sources of Personal Sense of Power

Structural Sources

Formal position, job level, control over resources, decision-making authority

Relational Sources

Quality of relationships, social support, network position, trusted advisor status

Psychological Sources

Past experiences, personality, self-perceptions, priming effects

The Power × Openness Interaction Matrix

Key Insights from the Interaction

  • High-power employees speak up regardless: Their activated BAS promotes voice even with closed leaders
  • Low-power employees are especially sensitive to leader cues: Their activated BIS makes them vigilant to signals of openness or closure
  • Leader openness matters most for those who need it most: The buffering effect is strongest for low-power employees
  • Double jeopardy: Low power + closed leader creates silence that is especially hard to break

Research Design

Study 1: Survey of Employees

Cross-sectional survey examining the relationship between personal sense of power, perceived supervisor openness, and self-reported voice behavior. Confirmed the predicted interaction: low-power employees showed more voice when supervisor was perceived as open.

Study 2: Experimental Manipulation

Laboratory experiment manipulating both sense of power (through recall task) and target openness (through vignette). Demonstrated causal effects: manipulated power and openness interacted to predict voice intentions.

Study 3: Replication with Different Context

Replicated findings in different organizational context with additional controls, ruling out alternative explanations and strengthening confidence in the approach-inhibition mechanism.

The Theoretical Foundation

Understanding the neuroscience of power and behavior

Keltner's Approach-Inhibition Theory

Dacher Keltner and colleagues (2003) proposed that power fundamentally transforms psychological states through its effects on two evolutionarily ancient neural systems:

The Evolutionary Logic

In ancestral environments, individuals with power (control over resources, physical dominance) could afford to approach opportunities and take risks—they had the resources to absorb losses. Those without power needed to be vigilant to threats from the powerful and cautious in their behavior. Natural selection shaped brains to automatically shift between approach and inhibition modes based on power cues.

The BAS: Behavioral Approach System

The BAS is activated by cues signaling reward, opportunity, and safety. When activated:

  • Positive emotions increase (joy, enthusiasm, desire)
  • Attention narrows to reward-relevant stimuli
  • Processing becomes automatic and heuristic
  • Behavior becomes disinhibited and assertive
  • Risk tolerance increases

The BIS: Behavioral Inhibition System

The BIS is activated by cues signaling threat, punishment, or uncertainty. When activated:

  • Negative emotions increase (anxiety, fear, vigilance)
  • Attention broadens to threat-relevant stimuli
  • Processing becomes controlled and systematic
  • Behavior becomes inhibited and cautious
  • Risk tolerance decreases
The Power-Behavior Connection: Having power (or feeling powerful) activates the BAS, producing approach behaviors including voice. Lacking power (or feeling powerless) activates the BIS, producing inhibition behaviors including silence. This is not a conscious choice—it's an automatic psychological response.

The Neural Pathway

From Power Perception to Behavior

EEG Evidence: Power in the Brain

Neuroimaging studies support the approach-inhibition theory. Research by Boksem et al. (2012) found:

  • Power priming increases left-frontal activity: The left frontal cortex is associated with approach motivation. Individuals primed with high power show increased left-frontal EEG activity.
  • Low power increases right-frontal activity: The right frontal cortex is associated with inhibition/avoidance. Low-power priming increases right-frontal activity.
  • These effects are automatic: Brain activation changes occur without conscious awareness of the power manipulation.

The Neural Mechanism

Power doesn't just change what people think—it changes how their brains process information at a fundamental level. The approach-inhibition shift happens below conscious awareness, which is why employees may feel inexplicably hesitant to speak up even when they consciously believe it would be safe.

The Moderating Role of Target Openness

The critical insight of Morrison et al. (2015) is that the power→behavior relationship is moderated by perceived target openness. Here's why:

For High-Power Individuals

BAS is already activated. They're in approach mode regardless of environmental cues. Leader openness matters less because internal power feelings drive behavior.

For Low-Power Individuals

BIS is activated, making them hypervigilant to environmental threats AND opportunities. Leader openness signals become highly salient and can shift behavior from inhibition to approach.

The Interaction Explained: Low-power individuals are in "surveillance mode"—their activated BIS causes them to carefully scan the environment for signals. When they detect leader openness, this can counteract BIS activation and enable voice. High-power individuals aren't scanning because their BAS makes them confident regardless of external signals.

Practical Implications

What leaders and organizations can do

For Leaders

Signal Openness Especially to Low-Power Employees

The research shows that your openness matters most for those with low personal sense of power. These employees are in "inhibition mode" by default and need clear signals that speaking up is safe. Generic "open door" policies are insufficient—active, targeted outreach is needed.

Actively Solicit Input

Don't wait for low-power employees to volunteer opinions. Directly ask for their perspectives in ways that feel safe and genuine.

Respond Positively to Early Voice

First impressions matter. If a low-power employee's initial attempt at voice is met negatively, their BIS activation will intensify.

Reduce Power Distance Cues

Physical setup, language, and behavior that emphasize hierarchy activate BIS. Reduce these cues to enable approach behavior.

Create Safe Channels

Anonymous feedback systems and intermediaries can provide voice opportunities that bypass the power-inhibition dynamic.

Recognize That Your Power Affects Others Automatically

Even if you're genuinely open and supportive, your formal power automatically activates BIS in subordinates. Your good intentions don't override their neural response to power cues. You must actively counteract this effect.

Strategic Framework for Leaders

Intervention Points in the Approach-Inhibition Cycle

For Organizations

Address Both Sides of the Equation

Two Intervention Targets:
  1. Increase employees' sense of power: Empowerment initiatives, participative decision-making, development opportunities
  2. Increase leader openness (and perceptions of it): Leader training, accountability for response to voice, feedback systems

Pay Special Attention to Power Asymmetries

The approach-inhibition framework suggests that voice will be systematically lower among:

  • New employees (who haven't built relational power)
  • Lower-level employees (less structural power)
  • Minority group members (may have lower sense of power due to stereotypes)
  • Contingent workers (less job security = less power)

Organizations should design voice mechanisms that specifically counteract these power disadvantages.

For Individuals

Understand Your Own Power Psychology

When you feel hesitant to speak up, ask: "Is this a rational assessment of risk, or is my BIS activated?" Recognizing the automatic nature of power-related inhibition can help you override it consciously.

Build Your Personal Sense of Power

  • Recall past successes: Research shows that recalling times you had power increases current sense of power
  • Build expertise and relationships: These provide alternative power bases beyond formal position
  • Adopt powerful body language: Expansive postures can increase subjective power feelings
  • Practice voice in low-stakes situations: Success experiences build sense of power

Assess Leader Openness Accurately

Your BIS may cause you to perceive leaders as less open than they actually are. Look for objective signals of openness rather than relying on your (potentially biased) feelings.
