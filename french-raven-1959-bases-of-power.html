üìö Core Argument

French and Raven's landmark chapter established the most influential taxonomy of power in social psychology. They identified five distinct bases from which social power derives, fundamentally shaping how we analyze influence in organizations, relationships, and society.

"The strength of power of O/P in some system a is defined as the maximum potential ability of O to influence P in a."

The key insight: power is not monolithic. Different types of power have different sources, operate through different mechanisms, and produce different outcomes. Understanding which base is being used helps predict both effectiveness and consequences.

üéØ Key Contributions

1. Differentiated View of Power

Before French & Raven, power was often treated as a single dimension. Their taxonomy showed that a manager might have high legitimate power but low expert power, or that a respected colleague might have high referent power without any formal authority. This differentiation enables precise diagnosis of power dynamics.

2. Source-Based Classification

Each power base has a distinct source:

  • Legitimate: Internalized values about proper authority
  • Reward: Control over valued outcomes
  • Coercive: Ability to administer punishment
  • Expert: Perceived knowledge or competence
  • Referent: Attraction and desire to identify

3. Foundation for Organizational Analysis

The framework became foundational for understanding workplace dynamics. It explains why formal authority alone often fails, why experts can influence without rank, and why charismatic leaders command loyalty beyond their formal role.

4. Later Extension: Informational Power

Raven later added a sixth base‚Äîinformational power‚Äîthe power that comes from possessing information others need, distinct from expert power because it's about specific information rather than general expertise.

üí° Theoretical Significance

The French & Raven framework provides the vocabulary for analyzing power in any social context:

Workplace Analysis

Diagnose why some managers succeed and others fail despite similar formal authority.

Leadership Development

Identify which power bases to cultivate for different leadership situations.

Resistance Strategies

Understand how to counter different types of power attempts.

üëî Legitimate Power

Power stemming from internalized values that give one person the right to prescribe behavior for another. The target accepts that the influencer has a legitimate right to influence them.

Sources

  • Formal organizational position
  • Cultural values (age, gender in some contexts)
  • Social structure and norms
  • Designation by legitimate authority

Characteristics

  • Range limited to scope of role
  • Depends on target's acceptance
  • Can be undermined by violation of norms
  • Stronger when supported by other bases

Example: A manager can direct work tasks (within role scope) but not personal life decisions (outside legitimate range).

üéÅ Reward Power

Power based on the ability to mediate rewards for another person. The target complies because they expect positive outcomes from the influencer.

Types of Rewards

  • Material: salary, bonuses, resources
  • Social: praise, recognition, inclusion
  • Opportunity: promotions, assignments
  • Psychological: approval, esteem

Effectiveness Factors

  • Target must value the rewards
  • Influencer must control rewards
  • Surveillance may be needed
  • Can create dependency

Example: A mentor who can provide career opportunities has reward power over prot√©g√©s who seek advancement.

‚ö° Coercive Power

Power based on the ability to mediate punishments. The target complies to avoid negative outcomes the influencer can impose.

Types of Coercion

  • Material: fines, demotions, firing
  • Social: criticism, ostracism
  • Physical: force or threat of force
  • Psychological: disapproval, rejection

Limitations

  • Requires constant surveillance
  • Breeds resentment and resistance
  • Only produces compliance, not commitment
  • Can damage relationship permanently

Example: A manager who threatens negative performance reviews has coercive power, but using it damages trust and morale.

üéì Expert Power

Power based on the perception that the influencer has special knowledge or expertise relevant to the situation. The target accepts influence because they believe the expert knows best.

Characteristics

  • Based on perceived competence
  • Limited to area of expertise
  • Can exist without formal authority
  • Highly persuasive when relevant

Building Expert Power

  • Develop genuine expertise
  • Demonstrate competence through results
  • Communicate knowledge accessibly
  • Stay current in your field

Example: A junior engineer with specialized knowledge can influence senior managers' technical decisions despite lower rank.

‚≠ê Referent Power

Power based on the target's identification with or attraction to the influencer. The target complies because they admire the influencer and want to be associated with them or gain their approval.

Sources

  • Personal charisma and likability
  • Shared identity or values
  • Admiration and respect
  • Desire for relationship

Unique Properties

  • Most broad in range of influence
  • Often unconscious identification
  • Can persist without surveillance
  • Foundation of charismatic leadership

Example: Team members adopt the work habits and values of a respected colleague they admire, even without being asked.

üîÑ How Power Bases Interact

In practice, power bases rarely operate in isolation. They interact, reinforce, and sometimes undermine each other.

Reinforcing Combinations

Legitimate + Expert

When a manager is also highly competent, their directives carry both formal authority and technical credibility. This is the ideal combination for knowledge work.

Legitimate + Referent

A well-liked manager with formal authority commands both compliance and genuine commitment. People follow because they should and because they want to.

Expert + Referent

A respected expert who is also personally admired becomes a powerful informal leader, even without formal rank.

Reward + Legitimate

Formal authority backed by control over meaningful rewards creates clear incentive alignment.

Undermining Combinations

Coercive Without Legitimate

Using punishment without accepted authority is seen as bullying. It provokes resistance and damages the relationship permanently.

Legitimate Without Expert (in technical roles)

A manager who lacks technical competence in a technical field loses credibility. Their legitimate authority is undermined by lack of expert power.

üìà Influence Outcomes

Kelman (1958) identified three types of influence outcomes that map to French & Raven's bases:

Compliance

Target conforms publicly but not privately. Produced mainly by reward and coercive power. Requires surveillance to maintain.

Bases: Reward, Coercive

Identification

Target conforms because of relationship with influencer. Produced by referent power. Lasts while relationship is valued.

Bases: Referent

Internalization

Target genuinely accepts influence as their own view. Produced by expert and legitimate power. Most durable form of influence.

Bases: Expert, Legitimate

üè¢ Organizational Applications

Manager Power Profile

Effective managers typically have:

  • High Legitimate: Clear role and authority
  • High Expert: Competence in their domain
  • Moderate Reward: Ability to recognize and reward
  • High Referent: Respect and likability
  • Low Coercive: Use punishment sparingly if at all

Power Without Authority

Individual contributors can build power through:

  • Expert power: Becoming the go-to person for specialized knowledge
  • Referent power: Building relationships and reputation
  • Informational power: Controlling access to critical information

Common Mistakes

  • Over-relying on legitimate power without building other bases
  • Using coercive power when other bases would be more effective
  • Failing to develop expert power in technical environments
  • Neglecting referent power (relationship building)
